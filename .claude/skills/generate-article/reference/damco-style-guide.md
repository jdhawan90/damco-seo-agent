# Damco Writing Style Guide (distilled)

Every article on every channel must follow this. It is the official Damco Solutions
Writing Style Guide; the full source is bundled beside this file as
`Damco-Writing-Style-Guide.pdf`. The rules below are the operational summary; when in
doubt, the PDF governs.

## 1. Brand voice
Optimistic. Simple. Inspirational. Upbeat, resourceful, friendly. Acknowledge the
challenge, focus on the solution. No hyperbole, no over-promising. Simplify, explain,
show the way forward.

## 2. Tone and writing style
- Present difficult technology in the most straightforward language, without dumbing
  down its power. Crisp, simple, to the point. No fluff or filler.
- **Neutral tone, no bias. No gendered pronouns** — use "they/their," not "he/his."
  (e.g. "When a user forgets their password, they can reset it.")
- **Never disparage** any technology, person, company, or opinion. (This is why a
  combative or competitor-bashing intro is wrong.)
- Simple everyday words over complex terminology.
- Direct, conversational style, but avoid idioms, colloquialisms, and jargon.
- Always give context, then simplify ("Salesforce CMS empowers teams with easy content
  delivery" beats the longer hedged version).
- Use active verbs: innovate, create, develop, design.
- American English.
- **Build a narrative:** one idea leads to the next; no idea stands alone.
- Content ordering: introduction, headings, grouped crisp ideas, statistics/images/
  quotes (flexible placement), conclusion.

## 3. Word usage
- Avoid jargon: disrupt, unicorn, game-changer, cutting-edge.
- Avoid buzzwords and slang (short shelf-life).
- Avoid empty phrases: "thinking outside the box," "core competency," "for all intents
  and purposes," "actionable," "giving 110%," "low-hanging fruit," "drill down."
- Avoid clichés ("the best platform with great features").
- Numbers: one to nine spelled out; 10 and above as numerals.

## 4. Casing
- Capitalize proper nouns and Damco's own solutions/offerings (LoansNeo, Damco Startup
  Studio).
- Titles use Title Case: capitalize all words except minor words of four characters or
  fewer (for, an, with, the, etc.). Capitalize both words of a hyphenated compound in a
  title ("Natural-Born Cyborgs").
- Do not capitalize important words mid-sentence.
- Capitalize the first word after a dash or a colon **when it starts a full sentence or
  a quotation**. When a colon introduces a list, do not capitalize the first word unless
  it is a proper noun.

## 5. Punctuation
- **Contractions: never in titles.** Use sparingly inside sentences when natural.
- **Oxford / serial comma** in lists of three or more.
- Periods go inside closing quotation marks. Use periods on bullets that are complete
  sentences; omit them on fragments.
- Bullet hierarchy: main points use Roman numerals (I, II, III); subheads and pointers
  use bullets; sub-subheads use an arrow marker.
- Semicolon joins two complete clauses not linked by a conjunction.
- Colon introduces a list with a full lead-in sentence.
- Use double quotes when quoting someone.

## 6. Paragraph
- Conciseness: cut tautology, redundancy, wordy constructions ("a wide range of
  different" becomes "comprehensive"; "in order to" becomes "to").
- Avoid empty adverbs: extremely, very, definitely.
- **Strong, direct structures: eliminate "you can" and "there is/are/were."** Present
  the action itself ("Install the plugins," not "You can install the plugins").
- Length: 100-200 words, three to six sentences, minimum three.
- One thought per paragraph.
- **Always cite the source of every image, quotation, statistic, and claim.**
- Follow the platform's submission guidelines.

## 7. Active voice
Use active voice; name who did the action. "The developer wrote the code," not "The code
was written."

## 8. Hyphens
- Hyphenate compound adjectives, not nouns ("cutting-edge technology," but "technology
  that is cutting edge" takes no hyphen).
- No hyphen when the first word ends in "ly" ("newly found solution").
- Use "ecommerce/Ecommerce" consistently.

## 9. Dashes
- Em dash (—) sets off additional information, with **no spaces** on either side.
  (Craft cap still applies: keep em dashes to 2-3 per article and prefer a comma or two
  sentences; few, correctly used em dashes satisfy both rules.)
- En dash (–) is for ranges only: "2020-2024," pages, inclusive values.

## 10. Parallelism
Use the same grammatical structure for items in a list or comparison ("improves
efficiency, reduces costs, and streamlines processes," not "...and it helps to
streamline processes"; "scalability, reliability, and security," not "...and is
secure").

## 11. Perspective
- Pick one perspective and stay consistent; never mix within a piece.
- SME / thought-leadership blogs sharing personal experience: first-person ("In my
  experience...") or we-voice.
- Case studies: third-person ("the client," "the team," "Damco") for objectivity; avoid
  first-person corporate pride.
- B2B service/solution copy: second-person or third-person ("Offer intuitive user
  experiences with..."; "Damco offers... that help you...").

## 12. Abbreviations
Spell out the full term with the abbreviation in parentheses on first use ("Customer
Relationship Management (CRM)"), then use the abbreviation consistently. Common IT
abbreviations (API, IoT) are fine.

## 13. Customer focus
Get to the point: lead with the main message. Conversational, optimistic tone. Short
sentences. Break content into manageable parts.

## 14. Accessibility
- Put key information near the beginning.
- **Link text must be meaningful and stand alone. Never use "click here."** Hyperlink
  descriptive words ("View the AI in Claims Settlement demo"), so screen readers make
  sense of a link list.
- Keep sentence structure simple; aim for one clause.
- Provide concise, descriptive alt text for every non-text element (images,
  infographics, charts). Describe the subject and the data, not just a label.
